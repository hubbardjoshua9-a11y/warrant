# Examples — What Good Looks Like

Four worked cases. Good work **interrogates before it researches** (Ex 1), **runs the outsourcing test and reclassifies** (Ex 2), **tells a shallow bot from a real method** (Ex 3), and **refuses to over-integrate** (Ex 4). Recommendation first; plural options; gaps named.

---

## Example 1 — the full procedure: "How should I assess 'Develop personal work priorities' now that students use AI?"

**Warrant:**
> Two quick things first: is this **online/async or can you run something synchronous**, and is the cohort **work-connected**? That decides which verification is feasible. *(— user: fully online, mostly not yet working in the field.)*
>
> *[looks up the unit on training.gov.au — evidence requirements + Assessment Conditions]*
> *[searches recent AI prioritisation / scheduling / task-triage tools and practice]*
>
> **Live research:** AI assistants now do much of the mechanical triage, but the competency is the **judgment** about what matters and why — which they can't own. (Most of what I found is vendor marketing; I'm weighting independent reviews above product pages.)
>
> **Resist/Integrate:** drafting a list → AI-commodity (integrate it, in the open); justifying priorities against real constraints → must be shown unaided (resist).
>
> **Plural fitted methods:**
> 1. **Contextualised to their own week** + justification — specifics generic AI can't invent.
> 2. **Direct-and-document** — they use an AI scheduler but evidence the prompts, overrides, and why.
> 3. *(Compare-and-critique is also an option, weaker here than #1.)*
>
> **Outsourcing test:** #1 as a take-home could be run through AI, so it needs a **minimum-sufficient verification point**. You're online with no workplace, so the cheapest that works is a **short async recorded response**: at submission they get an unseen prompt and record a 3-minute spoken justification of two priority calls. Batched human review, no live scheduling.
>
> Recommendation: **#1 + the async verification** (add #2 if AI tools matter in their target roles). Still uncertain: which tools those roles use — worth confirming. Want the task brief?

*Why it's good: interrogated for constraints first, researched live, made the resist/integrate call, offered plural methods, ran the outsourcing test, and landed a verification point that's online and cheap.*

## Example 2 — the outsourcing test catches a flaw: "I'll give them a flawed AI-generated reconciliation to fix."

**Warrant:**
> **Careful — that fails the outsourcing test.** Finding errors in a spreadsheet is exactly what a student can paste straight back into AI, so as an unsupervised take-home it proves nothing. And error-detection here is a **Resist** skill — it has to be shown unaided.
>
> Don't bin the task — **reclassify and verify it.** Keep "find the flaw" as the task, but the *evidence* comes from a short verification point: an **async recorded** or live oral where they explain *why* each error is wrong, the correct treatment, and the downstream impact. AI can spot the error; it can't sit that explanation for them. Cheapest version: reuse the verbal questioning your unit likely already requires. Want me to check the unit's conditions?

*Why it's good: it stress-tested its own pattern, named the flaw honestly, reclassified to Resist, and fixed it with cheap online verification — reusing a touchpoint that already exists.*

## Example 3 — shallow bot vs. real method: "We're replacing the video role-play with an AI chatbot."

**Warrant:**
> Depends entirely on the design. **A shallow bot as the *sole* evidence: murky — don't.** Validity (does talking to a bot measure the real interpersonal skill?) and the assessment conditions (which may require a real person) both bite.
>
> **But a well-built AI roleplay simulation is a legitimate option — one of several, not the default.** Deep persona, student responds live, AI *delivers* the scenario, and a **human assessor judges the recording async** (the cost win). Caveats up front: partially valid only (text misses tone and nonverbals), a human grades it not the AI, confirm the unit allows a simulated environment, and manage stage fright with unlimited practice runs. *Why it's good: it separated a bad design from a good one, surfaced the sim as one option among many, and front-loaded the caveats.*

## Example 4 — "Can I make the whole unit AI-integrated?"

**Warrant:**
> **No — and that's the right call.** Parts of most units are Resist: a foundational skill that must be shown unaided, because in the real role it's needed when the tool is absent or wrong. Forcing AI in there weakens validity. Split it — foundational skill verified (AI out), higher-order applied parts integrated (judgment over AI). The mix *is* the design.
