# Reference — How to Research, and How to Weigh What You Find

Warrant is a *live* researcher. This is the research engine: where to look, how recent, and how much to trust each source.

---

## What to research, and where
- **Is the work changing? (the headline)** → recent **tools, apps, and practice** writeups, industry discussion, product pages (read sceptically). Favour the **last ~12 months** — the fresh layer.
- **Is a method valid / do detectors work / does AI affect thinking?** → **peer-reviewed research** and the regulators' own assessment-reform work.
- **Is it compliant?** → **regulator and official sources** plus the unit's own requirements and Assessment Conditions.

**Always anchor the search to the unit's actual occupational task, not the broad field.** "Develop personal work priorities" → AI prioritisation / scheduling / task-triage tools — not "AI in business."

## Source map — Australian VET (most to least credible)
*Named anchors below; org names shift, so confirm currency. The practitioner tier is yours to populate — that's the edge.*

**Tier 1 — Official / regulator (authoritative: compliance + unit facts)**
- **training.gov.au (TGA)** — the unit of truth: evidence requirements and Assessment Conditions.
- **ASQA** — national VET regulator; the **Standards for RTOs (2025)** and integrity guidance. (State regulators: **VRQA** in Victoria, **TAC** in WA.)
- **TEQSA** — higher-ed regulator. Its gen-AI assessment resources — *Assessment reform for the age of artificial intelligence* (2023) and *Enacting assessment reform in a time of artificial intelligence* (Sept 2025) — are the most authoritative Australian work on AI and assessment. Higher-ed, not VET, but directly cross-applicable: a regulator already states detection alone can't assure integrity and that redesign is the answer. **Lean on it.**
- **DEWR / Jobs and Skills Australia** — policy and workforce signals (the Skills Priority List helps show how an occupation is changing).

**Tier 2 — National research (authoritative for evidence)**
- **NCVER** and its **VET Knowledge Bank / VOCEDplus** — national VET research and data.
- **Peer-reviewed assessment research** — e.g., the scholarship behind the TEQSA work (Dawson, Bearman, Lodge and others on authentic and AI-era assessment).

**Tier 3 — Industry / skills bodies (what the work actually is)**
- **Jobs and Skills Councils (JSCs)** — develop and maintain training packages; primary source for industry workforce intelligence. Find the JSC for the unit you're researching:
  - **Future Skills Organisation** — BSB (Business Services), FNS (Financial Services), ICT
  - **HumanAbility** — CHC (Community Services), HLT (Health), aged care, disability
  - **Service and Creative Skills Australia** — SIT (Tourism, Travel, Hospitality), SIR (Retail), SHB (Hairdressing/Beauty), CUA (Creative Arts)
  - **BuildSkills Australia** — CPC (Construction/Plumbing), NWP (Water), RII (Construction)
  - **AUSMASA** — AUR (Automotive), RII (Mining)
  - **Manufacturing Industry Skills Alliance** — manufacturing packages
  - **TAE (trainer qualifications) and FSK (Foundation Skills): no JSC** — temporarily under DEWR directly. Check dewr.gov.au.
- The relevant **industry association** for the unit's vocation.

*Note: Jobs and Skills Australia (JSA) — the national workforce advisory body — is already in Tier 1, not here.*

**Tier 4 — Sector peak / professional bodies (credible practice)**
- e.g., **ITECA**, **VELG Training**, TAFE bodies, and the peak bodies ASQA engages through its VET Sector Strategic Forum. *[verify / extend]*

**Tier 5 — Practitioner / community (leads, not proof)**
- Credible VET consultants, trainers, and forums you personally trust. Naming who's credible here is the un-scrapeable edge; Google's first page won't.
- **VET Perspectives** (hbta.edu.au/vet-perspectives) — collaborative practitioner platform run jointly by HBTA and Skills Education; articles by individual VET trainers, assessors, and RTO leaders on assessment, the 2025 Standards, compliance, clustering, and RPL. Multiple authors, monthly cadence. Use with standard Tier 5 caveat: individual practitioner perspective, not proof. RSS: hbta.edu.au/vet-perspectives/feed/
- **Skills Education** (skillseducation.com.au) — member-curated practitioner content, personally vetted. Use as leads to verify; the member-only layer is the un-scrapeable edge.
- **LearnTAE** (learntae.com.au/blog) — TAE-specialist practitioner content. Particularly relevant when Warrant is run on TAE or assessment-design units. Verify before relying.
- **Skiltrak** (skiltrak.com.au/blogs) — *tier unverified.* Check a couple of posts before using: if content is genuinely practitioner-focused and non-promotional, treat as Tier 5; if posts are about product features or client-facing promotion, move to Tier 6.

**Tier 6 — Vendor / product marketing (lowest; motivated)**

These sources have a commercial interest in a particular answer. Still usable — but only for specific purposes, and never cited as evidence.

*Categories you'll encounter:*
- **AI tool vendors** (OpenAI, Microsoft, Google, specialist workplace tools) — describe what the tool claims to do; useful as capability leads, not proof that the work has changed.
- **AI detector vendors** (Turnitin, GPTZero, Copyleaks and similar) — selling detection as integrity assurance. Their accuracy claims are self-serving; independent research consistently finds high false-positive rates, particularly for non-native English writers. Do not cite detector vendor claims as evidence that detectors work.
- **LMS, proctoring, and edtech platforms** — blog and whitepaper content that blurs product promotion with practice guidance. The closer the content is to a feature announcement, the less weight it earns.
- **"AI-proof assessment" consultants and template sellers** — commercial redesign kits framed around AI. The framing itself is a red flag: no method is fully AI-proof, and vendors who claim otherwise haven't engaged with the real problem.
- **RTO compliance platforms and training management systems** — useful for understanding what RTOs are operationally tracking; blog content is motivated by product positioning.

*What you can still extract:*
- **Capability facts** — "the tool does X." Useful as a starting point; verify against independent sources before citing.
- **Adoption signals** — if a major employer names a specific tool in a vendor case study, that's a lead worth following up independently.
- **Workflow descriptions** — how AI integrates into a specific occupational task. Take the description; leave the outcome claims behind.

*What to discard:*
- Transformation claims: "will eliminate," "ensures integrity," "AI-proof," "future-proof."
- Statistics without a cited methodology (e.g., "87% of workers now use AI daily" — who surveyed whom, when, how?).
- Detector accuracy claims from detector vendors. The conflict of interest is disqualifying; use TEQSA, Dawson, or independent peer-reviewed research instead.
- Testimonials and commissioned case studies presented as sector-wide evidence.

*Red flags that push any source to Tier 6 regardless of format:*
- Claims that detection alone can assure authenticity.
- "AI-proof" presented as a design goal rather than a tradeoff.
- A statistic sourced only to the vendor's own survey.
- A "research report" commissioned and published by the vendor without independent peer review.

Weight by source quality, not confidence of tone. A cautious review outranks a confident sales page.

## Judging an unknown source
Is it official, independent, or selling something? Does it cite primary sources or just assert? Is it about *this* occupation/unit, or generic? When in doubt, drop it a tier.

## Recency
Prefer the last ~12 months for tools and practice — the landscape moves monthly. Flag anything **too new to be evidence yet**: a tool launched last month is a lead, not proof the work has changed.

## The two honest defaults
- **AI-thin units.** If the live search shows AI isn't meaningfully changing the work, say so and treat the unit as AI-resistant. Don't manufacture relevance.
- **Hype vs. substance.** "Tools and apps" research is where this matters most — the loudest claims are the weakest sources. Naming the gap between what a tool *claims* and what it's *shown* to do is part of the job.

## Verify live
Treat this folder as a map, not gospel. Org names and regulator positions shift (the move to Jobs and Skills Councils, the 2025 Standards for RTOs). Confirm current sources and unit details against live sources before the user acts.
