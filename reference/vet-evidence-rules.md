# Reference — VET Evidence Rules & Key Concepts

The guardrails Warrant reasons within. These are stable principles; confirm the exact regulatory wording against current standards (see the maintainer note at the end).

---

## What's fixed vs. flexible
A unit of competency (from a nationally endorsed training package) specifies the **Elements, Performance Criteria, Performance Evidence, Knowledge Evidence, Foundation Skills, and Assessment Conditions.** These define **what must be demonstrated** — fixed, not the provider's to change.

The RTO develops its own **assessment tools and methods** — **how the evidence is gathered.** This is flexible, *within* the rules below. Warrant's entire scope lives here.

## The rules of evidence (V-S-A-C)
- **Validity** — the evidence actually addresses the unit's requirements.
- **Sufficiency** — there's enough evidence, across enough instances, to judge competence.
- **Authenticity** — the evidence is the **student's own work.** *This is the one AI strains most.*
- **Currency** — the evidence reflects current skills and knowledge.

## The principles of assessment (F-F-V-R)
- **Fairness** — adjustments for individual needs; the learner understands what's expected.
- **Flexibility** — methods suit the learner and context (this is the room you design in).
- **Validity** — sound, defensible judgments.
- **Reliability** — consistent judgments across assessors and occasions.

## Assessment Conditions — the boundary on method
The clause that can require observation, a workplace or simulated environment, supervision, or specific resources. It determines whether a given method (e.g., a chatbot standing in for a live role-play, or an unsupervised take-home task) is even permissible. **Always check it first.**

## AI detection: why "design beats detection"
Treat AI-text detectors as **unreliable controls, not evidence.**
- OpenAI withdrew its own AI-text classifier over low accuracy.
- Detectors produce false positives and have been shown to be **biased against non-native English writers** (e.g., Liang et al., 2023), which is a Fairness problem in its own right.
- Detection is adversarial and easily evaded; it doesn't establish authenticity.

The defensible response to AI is **assessment design** (resistant methods, integrated methods, and verification/triangulation) — not detection-and-penalty. A method that only works if you can catch cheating isn't a method.

## AI and critical thinking — handle as EMERGING
There's genuine, early concern that heavy AI reliance encourages cognitive offloading and weakens critical thinking. The evidence is **thin and emerging**, not settled. Warrant's design answer: where AI is integrated, make the **student's judgment over the AI** the assessable act, so the thinking is exactly what gets evidenced. Don't overclaim the research either way.
