  The live landscape — what AI is actually doing in this work

  This is not an AI-thin unit. Three things are genuinely shifting in WHS supervision right now, anchored to the unit's actual task:

  1. AI-assisted inspection and safety data platforms are now standard tools. SafetyCulture (Australian, widely used across manufacturing, logistics, retail, hospitality)
  launched significant AI features in 2025: AI-assisted incident investigation, advanced analytics dashboards, real-time hazard pattern recognition across sites, and an AI
  course builder. The supervisor's role in many workplaces is now less about recording hazards manually and more about interpreting AI-generated risk data and making judgment
  calls about controls. Element 5.2 (using aggregate data) is genuinely AI-native territory.

  2. AI is itself now a WHS hazard — and that's in legislation. NSW passed the Work Health and Safety Amendment (Digital Work Systems) Bill 2025 — an Australian first.
  Algorithms, AI tools, automated scheduling and surveillance systems are now regulated under WHS as potential hazards. PCBUs have a primary duty of care to ensure digital work  systems don't put workers at risk. This lands directly inside BSBWHS411: Elements 1 and 4 — communicating legislative provisions and identifying hazards. A contemporary
  scenario should include it. Flag: verify commencement and national reach — currently NSW only, other jurisdictions TBC.

  3. AI for training needs analysis and incident investigation. Platforms are replacing manual review of incident logs with automated trend analysis. Supervisors are
  increasingly expected to interpret AI-generated training gap data rather than build from scratch. Element 3.1 (identify training needs) is changing in workplaces that use
  these platforms.

  What isn't meaningfully changed by AI:
  - The consultation process (Element 2) — fundamentally interpersonal; must be demonstrated unaided
  - The hierarchy of control judgment (4.3–4.5) — AI can suggest controls, but the reasoning and justification under WHS law belongs to the supervisor
  - Legislative understanding (1.1) — AI can help look things up but is unreliable for legal accuracy; the supervisor must own the understanding

  Source quality note: SafetyCulture AI feature claims are Tier 6 (vendor marketing) — useful for scenario design but not cited as evidence the work has changed. The NSW
  legislative development is Tier 1. The AIHS article flagged in search was paywalled — not used.

  Plural fitted methods

  Method 1 (Recommended) — Scenario portfolio + direct-and-document + async recorded verification

  The scenario: A detailed fictional workplace (e.g., 20-person warehouse/distribution operation with a specific hazard profile, incident history, existing WHS policies, and an  organisational WHS register). Provided to the learner as their simulated work area. All 5 elements are assessed within this context.

  AI-integrated components (AI permitted openly):
  - Draft a toolbox talk using AI — submit the prompts used, the draft produced, corrections made, and written justification for each correction. The direct-and-document
  pattern. What's assessed is their judgment over the output, not the drafting.
  - Use provided incident and near-miss data to identify the team's WHS training needs — AI may assist the analysis; the learner annotates their reasoning and produces the
  training plan. Baseline-and-extend pattern.
  - Complete a hazard report using a provided digital inspection form (SafetyCulture-style template with scenario-specific hazards pre-populated for some fields, gaps left for
  others).
  - Maintain a simulated WHS record from scenario events; extract aggregate data and produce the required summary.

  Resist components (must show own reasoning):
  - Apply the hierarchy of control to two specific scenario hazards. Document every level considered, the control chosen, and the legislative basis. The scenario specifics make  generic AI responses less useful — but this still needs a verification layer.
  - Identify which WHS legislative provisions (Commonwealth and one state) apply to two scenario situations. Cite the actual provisions.

  Consultation evidence — Element 2 (the crux):

  Two options — present both to the assessor to choose based on capacity:

  Option A — AI roleplay simulation (async, no scheduling):
  AI plays two scenario team members — one raising a hazard they've noticed, one resistant to a new safe work procedure. Learner facilitates the consultation in real time,
  responds to both, documents outcomes. Human assessor grades the transcript against Element 2 criteria.
  Caveats: text misses nonverbals and tone; a human grades it, never the AI; provide unlimited practice runs. Regulator-dependent: whether an AI persona satisfies "interaction
  with others" under this unit's Assessment Conditions is not yet settled by ASQA. Option B is the cleaner fallback.

  Option B — Live video oral with assessor in role (scheduled, heavier):
  Assessor plays one team member (scripted role — raising a WHS concern, questioning a new procedure). Learner facilitates the consultation. Assessor can pivot to verbal
  questioning on hierarchy of control and legislative provisions in the same session. Satisfies "interaction with others" cleanly. Trade-off: scheduling load.

  Minimum-sufficient verification (anchors the whole portfolio):
  At submission, the learner receives one unseen scenario variation — a new hazard has appeared, or an existing control has been flagged as inadequate. They record an async
  5-minute spoken response: the hazard, the hierarchy of control they'd apply, the relevant legislative obligation, and how they'd consult the team. No scheduling; batched
  assessor review. Covers Elements 1, 2, 4, and 5 in a single pass. This is the authenticity anchor — unscripted, specific to an unseen prompt, hard to outsource in real time.

  Outsourcing test: The portfolio artifacts (toolbox talk, training plan, hazard report) could be AI-generated as take-home submissions without the verification layer. The
  direct-and-document pattern raises the bar but doesn't fully close it. The async recorded verification on an unseen prompt is what closes it.

  ---
  Method 2 (Alternative, lighter on design) — Same scenario portfolio + live video oral only

  Same portfolio structure, but the live video session (Option B above) does double duty: consultation evidence and verbal questioning on resist components. One 25-minute
  session replaces both the AI roleplay sim and the async recording. Cleaner on assessment conditions, higher scheduling cost. Better choice if the cohort is small and assessor  capacity is available.

  The contemporary scenario element worth building in

  Given the NSW Digital Work Systems Bill, add this to the scenario: the organisation is trialling an AI-based scheduling and productivity monitoring system for the warehouse
  team. Part of the assessment task is for the learner to identify whether the system introduces WHS risks (workload, surveillance, psychosocial), apply the hierarchy of
  control to those risks, and communicate findings to the work team. This tests Element 4 and Element 1 at a current legislative standard and makes the assessment authentically  contemporary. Verify commencement status and contextualise to NSW if needed.
